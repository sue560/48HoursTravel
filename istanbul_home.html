The Hagia Sophia

History

Originally a Christian temple, the Hagia Sophia was transformed into a mosque when the Sultan of the Ottoman Empire, Mehmet II, conquered Constantinople (now modern day Istanbul) in 1453.

The Hagia Sophia was restored several times: once in 1739 during the reign of Mahmoud I and in 1740 to create the foundation for ablutions. There is continued restoration today.

When the Turkish republic was founded in 1934, the mosque was transformed into a museum.

Did you know?

The Hagia Sophia has the second largest dome in the world, after the Parthenon in Rome.


The Blue Mosque

What to Expect

Just across from the Hagia Sophia is another magnificent mosque (although we're not quite sure why they call it the "blue" mosque as it is neither really blue from the outside or inside) with finely decorated dome.

Unlike the Hagia Sophia, the Blue Mosque is free to the public, and thousands of worshippers visit it on a daily basis. You can't miss the sound of its call to prayer every afternoon.

Men and women must enter separately, and there is a completely different entrance for tourists. Ladies, don't forget to bring your head scarves. If you did forget your favorite shawl at home today, no worries - the mosque provides hospital-blue scarves free of charge.

As a final tip, you might want to also bring some shoe deoderant as the tourist entrance of the mosque gets pretty smelly, as all visitors must take off their shoes before entering.


Topaki Palace

Beat the lines and get to the Palace early. Make your way to the end of the Palace.
